# Theoretical Framework

## Altenberg's Constructional Selection

The primary theoretical grounding comes from Lee Altenberg's "Genome Growth and the Evolution of the Genotype-Phenotype Map" (1995/2023).

### Key Connections to Our Work

**Bonner's Low Pleiotropy Principle vs Directional Selection**

Bonner (1974) argued low pleiotropy is necessary for evolvability — mutations affecting few traits are less likely lethal. Our static metrics confirmed this: direct encoding's low pleiotropy (87% neutrality, 2% large breaks) looks "safer."

But Altenberg distinguishes *stabilizing* selection (low pleiotropy wins) from *directional* selection (variation aligned with the selection gradient wins, even if pleiotropic). Our regime shift IS directional selection. Folding's high pleiotropy enabled the large phenotypic jumps that direct encoding couldn't achieve.

**Latent Directional Selection (Altenberg Section 2.7)**

Populations stuck on "constrained peaks" — appearing at a fitness maximum, but only because the G-P map can't produce the right variation. This is exactly direct encoding at fitness 0.10. Higher-fitness programs exist, but the representation creates a *kinetic constraint*. The "latent directional selection" is invisible until folding opens the right variational dimensions.

**The Genome as Population (Section 2.4)**

Altenberg proposes treating the genome as a population of genes, where genes with high "constructional fitness" proliferate. For our genotype strings: subsequences that fold into useful active sites should proliferate within evolved genotypes. This predicts measurable **motif enrichment** in evolved populations — a testable prediction (see Experiment 3.1).

**Type I and Type II Effects (Sections 2.4-2.5)**

- **Type I (genic selection)**: Genes that produce good duplicates proliferate within the genome. The planned Phase 3 genotype-level rewriting IS a Type I mechanism.
- **Type II (correlated allelic variation)**: Alleles of established genes tend to be adaptive because the gene's mode of action is correlated between origin and subsequent variation. Our folding chemistry creates Type II effects — a character's phenotypic contribution depends on fold neighbors, and fold topology is heritable.

**Wagner's Linear Model (Section 4)**

Three-layer model: genotype x -> phenotype y = Ax -> fitness. Our fold is a non-linear "A matrix." Under Gaussian stabilizing selection, new genes with low pleiotropy are favored. This predicts: under steady-state conditions (no regime shifts), folding should be at a disadvantage — confirmed by static metrics. The right G-P map depends on environmental stability.

## The Central Prediction

Altenberg's framework makes a clean, testable prediction for our system:

| Selection regime | Predicted winner | Reason |
|-----------------|-----------------|--------|
| Stabilizing (fixed target, 200 gens) | Direct encoding | Low pleiotropy avoids disrupting established fitness |
| Directional (shifting target, every 10 gens) | Folding | Pleiotropic mutations create the large jumps needed to track moving targets |

This is Experiment 2.3 — the single most theoretically informative experiment we can run.

## Protein Folding Analogy

The system is inspired by protein folding:

| Biology | Our System |
|---------|-----------|
| Amino acid chain | Genotype string |
| 3D folding based on local interactions | 2D folding based on fold instructions |
| Adjacent amino acids form active sites | Adjacent characters bond into program fragments |
| One mutation can restructure the fold | One character change can rearrange the grid |
| Neutral mutations in non-structural regions | Mutations in spacer/wildcard characters |
| Frameshift mutations from insertions/deletions | Insertion/deletion shifts downstream fold |

The analogy is structural, not chemical. We're not modeling physics — we're borrowing the key insight that **linear sequence -> spatial arrangement -> function** creates interesting evolutionary properties.

## Related Work

This project sits at the intersection of five research threads. The specific combination — string → spatial fold → local chemistry → executable symbolic program → coevolution — appears to be a novel recombination of several older ideas rather than a direct reimplementation of any one prior model.

### 1. Theory: GP-Map Evolution and Constructional Selection

- **Altenberg, L. (1995/2023)**. "Genome Growth and the Evolution of the Genotype-Phenotype Map." In *Evolution and Biocomputation*, Springer LNCS vol. 899, pp. 205-259. — The theoretical backbone. His "constructional selection" view is explicitly about evolution acting on the genotype-phenotype map itself and on the kinds of variation a representation makes available. Closest theoretical match for our shift from "evolve better programs" to "what developmental map produces better evolvability under different regimes?"
- **Wagner, G.P. (1989)**. "The origin of morphological characters and the biological basis of homology." — Linear G-P map model. Three-layer model (genotype → phenotype → fitness) predicts low pleiotropy wins under stabilizing selection — confirmed by our static metrics. The right G-P map depends on environmental stability.
- **Bonner, J.T. (1974)**. *On Development*. — Low pleiotropy principle. Our static metrics confirmed Bonner's prediction; our dynamic results show it fails under directional selection.
- **Manrubia et al., Nichol et al.** — Reviews on GP-map structure emphasizing that neutrality, phenotypic bias, robustness, and accessibility of variation shape evolutionary dynamics. Supports measuring these as properties of the map, not just of individual programs.
- **Pigozzi et al. (2024)**. "Quality-Diversity with Limited Resources." — Meta-learned developmental systems where evolution optimizes the G→P map itself for evolvability. Learned developmental processes produced more diverse artifacts and faster search. Direct computational evidence for Altenberg's thesis that the G-P map is a target of selection, not a fixed channel. Motivates our evolvable-chemistry direction (Direction 4 in `docs/future-directions.md`).
- **Srivastava, Louis & Martin**. "Predicting the Topography of Fitness Landscapes from the Structure of Genotype–Phenotype Maps." — Develops an analytical framework proving that two quantities derived from the GP map — neutral component (NC) sizes and their evolvabilities — completely determine expected fitness landscape ruggedness and peak heights under random fitness assignment. Validated across nine GP maps spanning RNA secondary structure, protein folding (HP models), self-assembly, and RNA-binding data, achieving Pearson correlations of 0.947–0.9996 between theory and simulation. Key results: (1) NC evolvability (distinct phenotypes reachable via single mutations from the NC boundary) is the primary predictor of landscape navigability; (2) NC size and evolvability are positively correlated across all tested systems — the authors term this "universal" — and this correlation suppresses the formation of large low-fitness peaks; (3) the geometric mean of NC evolvabilities determines a navigability threshold (Eq. 9): maps below this bound are analytically predicted to be non-navigable; (4) adaptive walks preferentially reach high-fitness peaks via NC-size bias ("arrival of the frequent"). The coarse-grained NC graph approach reduces a full genotype-space analysis to a much smaller abstract graph, making it computationally tractable. For chem-tape, the framework makes a direct prediction: enumerate NCs for small tape lengths (same folded program = same NC), compute evolvability per NC (how many distinct programs are reachable via single-token mutations), and use these to analytically predict whether a given decoder configuration produces navigable fitness landscapes. The proxy-basin traps observed in §v2.4 likely correspond to NCs with low evolvability — mutations within the proxy basin rarely produce structurally diverse programs, making the framework's navigability bound a concrete diagnostic. Limitations: the random fitness assumption does not capture correlated fitness functions used in chem-tape; the navigability bound relies on approximations (uniform NC connectivity, single evolvability per map) that may not hold for highly structured token-tape alphabets. (See `private/network-perspective-genotype-phenotype-mapping-gp-2020.pdf`.)
- **Sappington & Mohanty (2025)**. "Probabilistic Genotype–Phenotype Maps Reveal Mutational Robustness of RNA Folding, Spin Glasses, and Quantum Circuits." — Introduces probabilistic G-P (PrGP) maps that represent each genotype as a probability vector over phenotypes (capturing thermal or quantum uncertainty), enabling unified robustness analysis across RNA folding, spin-glass ground states, and quantum circuits. Derives a biphasic robustness scaling law ρ_n ≈ (k^ℓ/e^η ℓ log k)[f_n^ℓ(α + η log f_n)] where η ∈ [0,1] captures the degeneracy regime; validated with Pearson correlations 0.947–0.9996 across all three systems. Key findings: (1) rare phenotypes (small frequency f_n) exhibit suppressed robustness in the linear "DGP-like" regime; (2) common phenotypes saturate toward an upper robustness bound ρ^upper = 1 + (log f_n)/(ℓ log k); (3) genotype entropy S'_g — the spread of phenotypes produced by a genotype's mutational neighborhood — predicts accessibility of novel phenotypes; (4) heavy-tailed phenotype frequency distributions create "phenotypic bottlenecks" that restrict evolutionary access to rare but potentially adaptive structures. For chem-tape, measuring the phenotype frequency histogram (distribution of distinct program run-structures across 10,000 random tapes) directly tests whether the decoder produces heavy-tailed or uniform coverage. If heavy-tailed, the framework predicts that rare multi-chunk programs (which the proxy-basin experiments show are evolutionarily hard to reach) face suppressed mutational robustness — a representation-level explanation for the proxy-basin trap. Sweeping bonding threshold and top-K while measuring f_n and ρ would test whether the hand-crafted decoder is near an optimal robustness–evolvability trade-off. Concrete metric: genotype entropy S'_g per tape sequence, computed by mutating each position and tabulating program-structure diversity, then plotting ρ vs. S'_g. Limitation: theory assumes single-character mutations over a fixed alphabet; chem-tape's insertion/deletion operators require extension of the framework. (See `private/probabilistic-genotype-phenotype-maps-mutational-robustness-2025.pdf`.)
- **Mitchell & Cheney (2024–2025)**. "The Genome Instantiates a Generative Model of the Organism." — Proposes replacing the blueprint/program metaphor for the genome with a generative-model framing analogous to variational autoencoders (VAEs): the genome encodes a compressed latent representation that developmental processes decode into phenotypic form. The genome does not specify endpoint anatomy; it encodes constraints and initial conditions that channel self-organizing developmental processes driven by physics, chemistry, and cellular signaling — none of which are genetically encoded. This framing explains three properties simultaneously: (1) *robustness* — variation in individual latent variables is tolerated because the decoder compensates contextually, just as VAE decoders reconstruct coherent images from noisy latents; (2) *evolvability* — compression yields emergent modularity where orthogonal latent dimensions correspond to distinct phenotypic axes, enabling selection on one trait without universal pleiotropy; (3) *phenotypic novelty* — generative models can sample outside the training distribution, analogous to how genotype recombination produces organisms viable in novel environments not encountered during selection. The blueprint view fails because it presupposes isomorphic genome-anatomy mapping; the recipe view fails because it implies discrete, directional instruction sets that biology does not require. For chem-tape, this framing is directly instantiated: the token tape is a compressed latent representation, the bonding decoder is the decompressor, and the resulting postfix program is the reconstructed phenotype. A concrete prediction: chem-tape genotypes with shorter tapes (more compressed representation) should produce more robust phenotypic variation if the compression is structured rather than random, because the decoder must generalize across tape configurations to produce the same functional program. The framework also motivates studying the "latent structure" of evolved tape populations: if evolved tapes cluster in a low-dimensional subspace of tape space (a latent manifold), that suggests the developmental decoder has carved a favorable inductive bias for the target task class. Limitation: the framework is conceptual and analogical, not mechanistically precise — it guides experiment design but does not make quantitative predictions without additional modeling assumptions. (See `private/genome-instantiates-generative-model-organism-2024-2025.pdf`.)

### 2. GP-Map Structure: RNA and Neutral Networks

The strongest biological analogue to our folding layer. In RNA, a linear sequence maps to a folded secondary structure, and the structure of that map produces neutrality, robustness, phenotypic bias, and constrained innovation.

- **Dingle, Louis et al.** — RNA GP-map structure strongly constrains evolution. Phenotypic bias and accessibility properties of the map shape evolutionary outcomes. Most relevant for interpreting our neutrality, pleiotropy, and regime-shift findings.
- **Wagner, A.** — Robustness and evolvability via neutral networks. Uses RNA as a core example of how neutral networks coexist with innovation. Our static-vs-dynamic discrepancy (high neutrality looks good statically but is inertia dynamically) maps directly to this framework.
- **Kimura, Huynen** — Neutral evolution theory and how neutral mutations enable exploration of genotype space.

### 3. GP Precedents: Indirect Encodings and Dynamic Environments

- **O'Neill & Ryan** — Grammatical Evolution. Closest GP precedent on the "linear genome indirectly yields executable program" axis. GE uses a grammar-based mapping with neutrality via redundant codons. Our system differs because indirection is spatial and chemistry-like rather than grammatical.
- **Miller** — Cartesian Genetic Programming. Close match on neutrality, inactive genetic material, and many-to-one mappings. Our "spacers / junk-DNA-like regions" are in the same family of ideas, though our phenotype is assembled by spatial adjacency rather than indexed graph decoding.
- **Stanley** — Artificial embryogeny / developmental encodings. Taxonomy of indirect encodings where the genome drives a developmental process. Conceptually close to our fold-and-bond pipeline, though most of that literature grows graphs, circuits, or neural networks rather than symbolic programs.
- **Banzhaf & Bakurov (2024)**. "On the Nature of the Phenotype in Tree Genetic Programming." — Develops a simplification algorithm that extracts the true effective phenotype from Tree GP genotypes by identifying and removing semantically ineffective code, using semantic similarity matrices (Mean Absolute Deviation of program outputs on training instances) to identify coalescing subtrees. Validated across five regression benchmarks. Key quantitative results: (1) genotypes grow to 100+ nodes by generation 80 while exact phenotypes stabilize at 15–25 nodes — a 4–7× genotype-to-phenotype size ratio; (2) approximate phenotypes (using a percentile threshold t controlling distance-based coalescing) achieve 10–20× reduction; (3) counterintuitively, rougher approximations (larger t) yield *better* population fitness than exact phenotypes, suggesting ineffective code provides beneficial regularization or protective buffering against fitness loss; (4) true phenotypic diversity (unique phenotype count) converges to 15–25 distinct structures by mid-evolution, far below apparent genotypic diversity — standard genotypic diversity metrics dramatically overestimate true exploration. For chem-tape, the effective phenotype is the subset of run tokens that survive the top-K bonding filter and contribute to program execution; non-bonded segments and malformed runs below the minimum length threshold are the chem-tape equivalent of ineffective Tree GP nodes. The finding that rougher approximations outperform exact ones directly suggests the top-K extraction threshold should be swept: too tight discards beneficial neutral material; too loose allows excessive noise. The prediction that true phenotypic diversity is far lower than genotypic diversity is actionable: if evolved tape populations show only 15–25 unique program structures despite high genotypic diversity, it confirms that neutral material is widespread and that diversity-maintenance operators (lexicase selection, niching) should target phenotypic rather than genotypic distance. Replicable experiment: measure unique-program-structure count per generation across evolutionary runs and compare to genotypic diversity; the ratio reveals the effective neutral-network coverage. (See `private/modern-gp-research-phenotype-simplification-2024.pdf`.)
- **O'Neill et al. (GECCO 2011)** — "Dynamic Environments Can Speed Up Evolution with Genetic Programming." Explicitly tested the Kashtan & Alon hypothesis inside GP using "structurally varying goals" (SVG) where tasks share subproblems. Key comparison point: they tested environmental variation as a property of the task; we test it as an interaction with the representation. Their result was about convergence speed; our result is about representational accessibility (direct encoding never discovers the solution, not just slowly).

### 4. Evolutionary Dynamics: Coevolution and Changing Environments

- **Hillis, W.D. (1990)**. "Co-evolving parasites improve simulated evolution as an optimization procedure." — Foundational reference for coevolution as a way to escape local optima. Direct precedent for our solver/tester/oracle ecology.
- **Zaman et al. (2014)** — "Coevolution Drives the Emergence of Complex Traits and Promotes Evolvability." Host-parasite coevolution in Avida leads to more complex digital organisms vs static environments. Our bond count trajectory is analogous to their instruction count / logic depth metric. Our complexity ceiling at 3 bonds mirrors the early plateau pattern in Avida experiments before scaffolding emerges. Key difference: Avida does not vary the genotype-phenotype map.
- **Lenski et al.** — "The Evolutionary Origin of Complex Features." Complex traits evolve through incremental scaffolding of simpler ones (NOT → NAND → AND → EQU). Our chemistry has a similar scaffolding structure (get+key → comparator → fn predicate → filter) but evolution currently stalls at count+rest. This suggests the complexity ceiling is a property of the developmental map, not just insufficient search time.
- **Kashtan & Alon** — Modularly varying goals. Varying environments speed evolution and promote modular structure. Natural comparison for our regime shift result. Previous work showed that modularly varying environments accelerate evolution in neural networks, logic circuits, and some GP systems — but those studies treated the genotype-phenotype mapping as fixed. Our result shows that the choice of developmental encoding fundamentally alters the system's ability to respond to environmental shifts.
- **Clune, Mouret, Lipson** — "The Evolutionary Origins of Modularity." Changing environments cause evolution to produce modular structures. Complements Kashtan & Alon.
- **Gupta et al.** — Coevolution deforming fitness landscapes to open new innovations. Precedent for our coevolution designs, though our "single shared representation with fluid roles" is a more representation-centric twist.
- **Kauffman, S.A. (1989)**. "Adaptation on rugged fitness landscapes." — NK adaptive landscape model.
- **Evolvability ES (2018/2019)**. "Evolvability ES: Scalable and Direct Optimization of Evolvability." — Introduces Evolvability ES, a gradient-based meta-learning algorithm (MAML-inspired, using stochastic computation graphs) that directly optimizes evolvability — the propensity of genomes to generate phenotypic diversity through mutation — as a first-class objective. Two variants: MaxVar-EES (maximizes trace of behavior-characteristic covariance across mutated offspring) and MaxEnt-EES (maximizes distribution entropy via kernel density estimation). Key results on continuous control: evolvability-optimized populations discover polymodal behavioral distributions (policies moving in nearly all directions) while standard ES learns primarily forward locomotion; evolvability-optimized genomes adapt to new tasks within 5–20 generations vs. 50+ for standard ES, approaching performance of populations trained from scratch. Crucially, no additional domain evaluations are needed beyond what standard ES already performs — gradients are computed over the same ~10,000 policies per generation. For chem-tape, this framework provides a concrete optimization target for the evolvable-chemistry direction. Instead of selecting decoder configurations by immediate fitness, optimize them by offspring behavioral diversity — the distribution of program structures produced by mutating a tape with the given decoder settings. A chem-tape behavior characteristic vector could encode: run count, operator-frequency histogram, token diversity, and program output range. High evolvability by this measure directly predicts fast regime-shift adaptation. The proxy-basin trap (§v2.4) corresponds precisely to low-evolvability regions: mutations within a proxy basin produce structurally similar programs, and MaxVar-EES would specifically select against such configurations because they exhibit low offspring variance. Concrete experiment: compute MaxVar evolvability for current decoder configurations on the §v2.6 Pair 1 task family; measure whether the proxy-basin-attractor configuration has measurably lower evolvability than configurations that escape it. Limitation: the approach assumes continuous smooth behavior spaces; chem-tape's discrete symbolic programs require careful design of behavior metrics and may require surrogate differentiable loss functions analogous to those used for discrete RL policies. (See `private/evolvability-behavior-landscapes-2023.pdf`.)

### 5. Mechanism: Fold-Dependent Assembly of Executable Structure

- **Morris, H. — Typogenetics** (from Hofstadter's *Gödel, Escher, Bach*). Closest historical precursor. A strand encodes operations on itself, and the strand's derived "enzyme" has a 2D tertiary structure with left/right/straight folding inclinations; that folded structure determines binding preference and where operations act. Overlaps on: linear symbolic sequence, folding-derived structure, local binding/adjacency effects, executable consequences, ALife framing. Key difference: Typogenetics is about self-transforming/self-reproducing strands; our system uses a folded string to assemble a *separate* executable symbolic phenotype via local chemistry rules. Self-modification vs program synthesis.
- **Fontana & Buss — Algorithmic Chemistry (AlChemy)**. Molecules are computational objects whose interactions generate new computational objects. Strong precedent for our "chemistry yields computation" framing, but not spatial in the same way and does not use folding-derived geometry to assemble programs.
- **Buliga — chemlambda / molecular computers**. Molecules as graphs of atoms and bonds, computation via local graph rewrites interpreted as chemical reactions. Close to our "chemistry rules assemble behavior" intuition, but the executable structure is already graph-like, whereas our system starts from a 1D sequence that must first acquire geometry through folding.

### 6. Developmental System Design: Mechanism Ablation

- **Kuyucu, Trefzer, Miller & Tyrrell (2011)**. "An Investigation of the Importance of Mechanisms and Parameters in a Multicellular Developmental System." *IEEE Trans. Evolutionary Computation*. — Systematic ablation study of a GRN-based multicellular developmental system across six pattern-formation tasks. Key results relevant to chem-tape and folding: (1) Local contact signaling is essential; removing it collapsed performance to 0/50 on some tasks. (2) Constant diffusion is harmful; the best diffusion mechanism was an evolvable diffusion protein (gene-controlled, not hard-wired). (3) Extra chemicals and mechanisms (local proteins, messenger molecules, food, unproductive genes) did *not* produce overall improvement — minimality may be an advantage. (4) Parameter optima (production/consumption rates, activation thresholds) are problem-dependent; evolving them per gene was the best compromise. (5) The structuring-protein phenotype mapping (reading transient developmental dynamics) significantly outperformed reading final chemical concentrations. (6) Switching the gene activation decision rule from conjunctive to voter caused large negative effects — small logic choices reshape the search landscape. (7) Results from one task family do not generalize; testing across multiple pattern classes is necessary for credible claims. Methodological takeaway for chem-tape: systematic micro-ablation of decoder rules (bonding logic, run termination, malformed-fragment handling) across multiple task families, following this paper's approach, is a concrete research direction. The paper's strongest chem-tape parallel is that local adjacency structure is the key signal carrier; its weakest is the temporal-dynamics finding, since chem-tape's single-pass decoder lacks the iterative developmental steps that make transient behavior informative in their GRN system.

### Novelty Assessment

The general idea is not unprecedented; the nearest ancestor is Typogenetics. But the specific use of protein-like folding as a genotype-phenotype map that assembles runnable symbolic programs appears to be a novel combination. No prior system we found implements the full chain: string genome → fold into space → adjacency/bond rules → assembled executable symbolic program. The literature has pieces in separate traditions: folding and binding in artificial genetics (Typogenetics), chemistry as computation (AlChemy), local graph rewrites (chemlambda), digital organism dynamics (Avida).

### Positioning Summary

This project is closest to RNA-style genotype-phenotype map research and indirect/developmental encodings in evolutionary computation, with coevolutionary pressure borrowed from Hillis/Avida-style adversarial evolution. Its main novelty is replacing grammar or graph decoding with a protein-inspired fold-and-bond developmental process that assembles symbolic programs from local spatial interactions. The strongest comparison axes are:

| Result | Compare against |
|--------|----------------|
| Static metrics vs dynamic adaptation | RNA GP-map, robustness/evolvability theory |
| Direct vs indirect encoding | GE, CGP, developmental encoding surveys |
| Regime shift response | O'Neill GECCO 2011, Kashtan & Alon |
| Coevolution for escaping plateaus | Hillis, Zaman/Avida, Gupta |
| Complexity ceiling as map property | Lenski/Avida scaffolding, Zaman complexity trajectories |
| Fold-dependent program assembly | Typogenetics, AlChemy, chemlambda |

## Meta-Learned Developmental Systems

Recent work on meta-learning evolvability provides a modern computational frame for Altenberg's constructional selection. Instead of evolving solutions directly, evolution learns *how to generate solutions* via developmental systems (e.g., neural cellular automata). The developmental system itself is optimized to maximize evolvability — producing more diverse artifacts and enabling faster, more robust search.

- **Pigozzi et al. (2024)**. "Quality-Diversity with Limited Resources." — Demonstrates that learned developmental systems (genome → developmental process → phenotype) outperform direct encodings on diversity and search efficiency when the developmental process is itself under selection pressure. The key insight: optimizing the G→P map *for the quality of variation it produces* — not just for the fitness of its current output — yields representations that are inherently more evolvable. (See `private/meta-learner.pdf` for extended analysis.)
- **Moreno, Banzhaf & Ofria (GECCO 2018)**. "Learning an Evolvable Genotype-Phenotype Mapping." — Presents AutoMap, a methodology for automatically learning G-P mappings via neural network autoencoders trained on phenotypes harvested from local fitness peaks of a preliminary direct-encoding evolutionary search. Two variants: a *bottleneck* autoencoder (only the decoder serves as the G-P map; genotypes are low-dimensional bottleneck codes) and a *denoising* autoencoder (the full trained network serves as the G-P map, correcting noisy genotypes toward fit phenotypes). Training protocol: run 250 populations × 300 individuals × 50 generations under direct encoding, harvest champion phenotypes. Key results: in the n-legged table domain, bottleneck mapping reaches target fitness within 100 generations vs. ~950 (near initialization) under direct encoding; in Scrabble, the denoiser achieves ~85% character prediction accuracy and produces faster fitness gains with simultaneously greater novelty. Critically, analysis of what the autoencoders *learned* is more important than raw performance: the bottleneck decoder discovered the underlying task symmetry (all 100 leg lengths encode the same scalar), effectively discovering a 1D sufficient statistic; the denoiser learned spell-checking — repairing multiple character sites when a single mutation is introduced, a form of canalizing correction that produces high multi-site novelty from single-site mutations. For chem-tape, the denoising approach is most directly applicable: a denoiser trained on champion tape phenotypes would learn which partial folding patterns robustly produce valid postfix programs, and could correct partially-damaged tapes rather than producing malformed runs. This directly addresses the malformed-fragment handling problem and potentially discovers structural regularities in valid run compositions that the hand-crafted decoder misses. The key design question is what "noise" to inject during training: adding random token substitutions mirrors point mutation; adding insertion/deletion noise trains the denoiser to handle the genetic operators actually used. Limitation: training cost requires GPU resources (20,000 populations for the Scrabble domain), making this a one-time investment per task family rather than an online learning approach; the trained mapping may not generalize if the target fitness landscape changes significantly (regime-shift experiments would require retraining or online adaptation). (See `private/learning-evolvable-genotype-phenotype-mapping.pdf`.)
- **(2024)**. "Searching Search Spaces: Meta-evolving a Geometric Encoding for Neural Networks." — Presents a two-level meta-evolution for automatically discovering optimal distance functions for GENE (Geometric Encoding for Neural-network Evolution), an indirect encoding that places neurons in a latent geometric space and computes weights from distance functions, reducing genome dimensionality from O(n²) to O(n). Outer loop: CGP (32 population, 5000 generations, 64 nodes, 12 operators) evolves candidate distance functions; inner loop: Evolution Strategies evaluates each function by training neural policies on continuous control tasks (HalfCheetah, Walker2D). Meta-evolution fitness combines task performance (β=1/3), network-property regularization (weight distribution, input utilization), and a bonus for using all input dimensions. Key results: the best evolved function LD-367 achieves 7766 vs. 1501 for the hand-crafted pL2 baseline on HalfCheetah (5.2× improvement) and 2211 vs. 1754 on held-out Hopper — demonstrating generalization to unseen tasks. Most striking finding: LD-367 simplifies to (x2 > z1) × y2, acting as an adaptive pruning operator that sets many weights to exactly zero; sparsity was not explicitly incentivized yet emerged because sparse networks generalize better across environments. This demonstrates that meta-evolution discovers structural properties (sparsity, modularity) that human designers miss when hand-crafting encodings. For chem-tape, the two-level architecture maps directly: outer loop uses CGP or grid search to evolve decoder rules (bonding threshold, top-K K, run-length minimum, token-valence assignments); inner loop evaluates each configuration by running full evolutionary runs on symbolic regression or predicate-filtering tasks. The emergent-sparsity finding predicts that meta-evolved chem-tape decoders may discover implicit token specialization — e.g., only 3–4 of the available token types are functionally useful in high-fitness programs — a testable structural signature visible in the token-frequency distribution of evolved populations. The regularization terms (network property penalties) have direct chem-tape analogues: penalize very short or very long run distributions, reward token diversity, ensure programs use both operator and operand types. Limitation: the nested evaluation is described by the authors as "hardly tractable"; each outer-loop evaluation requires a full inner ES training run, making full meta-evolution feasible only with short inner runs or small populations. The systematic ablation approach of Kuyucu et al. (Section 6 above) provides a cheaper complementary strategy: manually sweep decoder parameters first to identify the gradient direction before committing to full meta-evolution machinery. (See `private/searching-search-spaces-meta-evolving-geometric-encoding-neural-networks-2024.pdf`.)

### Connection to Altenberg

This is a computational realization of Altenberg's core thesis: the G-P map is not a fixed channel but a target of selection. Altenberg's Type I effects (genic selection — genes that produce good duplicates proliferate) predict exactly what meta-learned developmental systems achieve: the developmental rules that produce diverse, fit offspring are the rules that survive. The difference is resolution — Altenberg analyzed this at the level of individual genes within a genome; meta-learner architectures optimize the entire developmental pipeline as a unit.

### Connection to Chem-Tape

Chem-tape is a fixed developmental system: token tape → bonding rule → run segmentation → postfix program. The bonding rule, extraction logic (top-K), and bond-protection parameter are hand-designed constants. This meta-learner framing suggests a natural experiment: **evolve the developmental rules themselves**.

The architecture becomes two-level:

```
meta-genome  →  chem-tape decoder (bonding threshold, K, bond-protection, min-run-length, ...)
genome       →  phenotype (via the decoded rules)
```

The 1.8-era `DevGenome` experiment (see `docs/folding/findings.md` §Evolvable Chemistry) attempted this with population-level chemistry parameters but failed to improve fitness. Two design flaws explain the gap:

1. **Granularity.** DevGenome was shared across the entire population. Meta-learner architectures attach developmental parameters to each individual, so chemistry variation is heritable and subject to individual-level selection.
2. **Selection target.** DevGenome was selected on immediate fitness only. The meta-learner insight is to select for *evolvability* — e.g., offspring diversity, adaptation speed after task switches, or Pareto criteria on (fitness, variation-quality). A chemistry that produces diverse offspring survives environmental shifts even if its peak fitness is slightly lower.

The proxy-basin-attractor finding (§v2.4 arc in `docs/chem-tape/README.md`) provides a concrete test case: both BP_TOPK and Arm A get trapped in single-predicate basins. An evolvable chemistry that biases against easy-to-assemble proxy programs — or that favors multi-chunk compositions — could reshape the fitness landscape to escape these basins, addressing the trap at the representation level rather than through sampler redesign.

**Risk:** Chem-tape's bonding rules are discrete and combinatorial, unlike the smooth differentiable developmental systems in neural-CA meta-learners. The selection signal for evolvability (offspring diversity → better long-term fitness) is noisier with discrete rules. The DevGenome negative result is consistent with this concern. A diagnostic grid sweep over chemistry parameters on existing tasks (does *any* parameterization lift §v2.6 Pair 1 from 4/20?) would test whether the chemistry search space has enough useful structure before committing to full meta-learning machinery.

## Open Theoretical Questions

1. **Does constructional selection operate on genotype strings?** If motif enrichment is observed (Exp 3.1), it supports Altenberg's genome-as-population model applied to our linear genotype representation.

2. **Is there an optimal pleiotropy level?** Altenberg discusses the tradeoff but doesn't specify an optimum. Our system could sweep pleiotropy (by varying fold instruction density) and measure evolvability.

3. **Does the G-P map itself evolve?** If evolved genotypes have lower pleiotropy than random genotypes (Exp 3.2), evolution has shaped the map — not just the programs the map produces.

4. **Can rewriting create Type I constructional selection?** Phase 3 (genotype-level rewriting) would let programs edit each other's genotypes. If successful rewriters proliferate, this is constructional selection operating through program-on-program evolution.

## References to Obtain

The following papers are closely related but PDFs not yet downloaded:

- **Compact Linear Genetic Programming with Surrogate-Assisted Local Search (2024)**. Proposes a compact linear GP representation that reduces genome size, improves building-block reuse, and maintains multiple programs within one representation. Relevant as a comparison point for chem-tape's linear genome approach.
- **Meta-Learning an Evolvable Developmental Encoding (ALIFE / arXiv 2024)**. Evolves the developmental system itself using neural cellular automata that read a genome and grow structures. Learned developmental encodings become increasingly evolvable; search becomes faster and more diverse. Direct parallel to the chem-tape evolvable-decoder direction.
- **Evolvability ES / Quality Evolvability ES (2019–2021)**. Algorithms that explicitly optimize evolvability (ability to produce diverse useful offspring) instead of fitness. Evolution selects for high offspring diversity; key insight is that evolution can discover representations that are easier to evolve. Motivates measuring evolvability of chem-tape genomes directly.
- **Representation-Operator Interaction (GECCO 2024–2025)**. Modern representation research showing that locality, redundancy, and neutrality emerge from the interaction of representation and mutation operators — a representation cannot be evaluated independently of its operators. Directly applies to studying mutation operators + bonding rules + run segmentation as a combined system.
- **Emerging Theoretical Work on Evolvable Design (2025)**. Proposes that evolvable systems share three properties: hierarchical structure, uneven phenotype distribution, and modular composition of substructures. Chem-tape naturally exhibits all three.
- **Hinton, G.E. & Nowlan, S.J. (1987)**. "How Learning Can Guide Evolution." *Complex Systems* 1, 495–502. The foundational Baldwin-effect computational demonstration: plasticity converts a needle-in-a-haystack flat landscape into a slope because partial-correct agents gain proportional fitness via learning. **Known parametric flaw** (see Theory, Evolution, and Games Group critique): the choice of 1000 agents × 1000 trials × 20 letters is meticulously tuned so at least one agent is near-solved at initialization and learning fills in the rest — the result is parametrically guaranteed rather than generically demonstrated. For §v2.5-plasticity-1a the structural analogue is AND with only 4 truth-table rows: any plasticity mechanism with ≥2 bits of per-row state trivially memorizes the full task, so the hold-out split is necessary but may not be sufficient. Stronger design implication: parametrize a task family (e.g., `THRESHOLD(k)` for k ∈ [2..10]), evolve the *mechanism*, let plasticity adapt *k* per-evaluation, and measure whether plasticity increases evolvability across the family rather than fitness on any single instance. Pre-register the null explicitly: plasticity may simply accelerate settling into the §v2.4 proxy-basin attractor (`max > 5`), which would be a Baldwin-negative result worth logging per methodology.md.
- **Mayley, G. (1996)**. "Landscapes, Learning Costs, and Genetic Assimilation." *Artificial Life* 2(4), 495–519. Formal follow-up to Hinton & Nowlan identifying when Baldwin → genetic assimilation vs when it stalls in a "shielded evolution" regime. Key result: learning must have a measurable *cost* (time, energy, or error during acquisition) for evolution to have a gradient to absorb the learned trait genetically; costless learning prevents assimilation entirely. For chem-tape v2.5, this predicts that plasticity must have a measurable bond-cost or compute-cost differential between learned and innate solutions — otherwise assimilation will not occur and the experiment cannot distinguish learning-as-scaffold (Baldwin proper) from learning-as-permanent-substitute (no evolutionary pressure to internalize). Makes the plasticity-cost parameter a load-bearing design variable, not an aesthetic one.
- **Dennett, D.C. (1995)**. *Darwin's Dangerous Idea*. Treats Hinton & Nowlan charitably as an *existence proof* for the Baldwin mechanism rather than a quantitative demonstration — a useful framing distinction when citing the original paper without inheriting its parametric-rigging problem.
